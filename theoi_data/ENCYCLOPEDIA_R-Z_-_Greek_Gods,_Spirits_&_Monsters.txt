      THEOI HALIAN
     </a>
     (Theoi Halioi) The gods of the sea. They were ruled by the sea-kng Poseidon.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="rustic-gods.html">
      THEOI NOMIAN
     </a>
     (Theoi Nomioi) The gods of the countryside, the pastures and the wilds. Most of these were minions of Dionysus the lord of vegetation, Artemis queen of beasts, and Hermes god of herds.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="olympian-gods.html">
      THEOI OLYMPIAN
     </a>
     (Theoi Olympioi) The twelve, great Olympian gods (and their offspring) who presided over the various facets of ancient Greek civilisation, society and culture.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="sky-gods.html">
      THEOI URANIAN
     </a>
     (Theoi Ouranioi) The gods of the sky. They were in the service of Zeus and Hera, the king and queen of the heavens.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="titans.html">
      THEOI TITANES
     </a>
     The first generation of gods. They were largely superseded by the younger Olympians and were primarily associated with time and prophecy.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="bestiary.html">
      THERES
     </a>
     The ancient greek word for "monsters" and "beasts".
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/TheresAithiopikoi.html">
      THERES, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Theres Aithiopikoi) Fantastic creatures believed to inhabit the distant Ethiopia (Sub-Saharan Africa) including Pegasi, Dragons, Sphinxes, and the Catoblepas, Yale, Leucrocota, and Amphisbaena.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/TheresIndikoi.html">
      THERES, INDIAN
     </a>
     (Theres Indikoi) Fabulous beasts thought to inhabit the distant land of India.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThero.html">
      THERO
     </a>
     The nymph who nursed the infant god Ares.
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="heading">
      THESEUS
     </span>
     The great Athenian hero who slew the Minotaur of Crete.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Protogenos/Thesis.html">
      THESIS
     </a>
     The primordial goddess of creation and genesis.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThespia.html">
      THESPIA
     </a>
     (Thespeia) A Naiad-nymph daughter of the river Asopus and eponym of the town of Thespia who was carried off by the god Apollo.
    </p>
    <figure class="image-main image-main-400">
     <a href="../Pontios/NereisThetis.html">
      <img alt="Thetis" class="img-responsive center-block" src="../image/Z33.2Nereides.jpg"/>
     </a>
     <figcaption>
      <a href="../Pontios/NereisThetis.html">
       THETIS
      </a>
     </figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p>
     <a href="../Pontios/NereisThetis.html">
      THETIS
     </a>
     The leader of the fifty Nereids. The hero Peleus wrestled her on the sea-shore and held her fast, despite her shape-shifing, until she agreed to become his wife. Their son Achilles was the greatest of the heroes of the Trojan War.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThisbe.html">
      THISBE
     </a>
     The eponymous nymph of the Boeotian town of Thebes.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Gigante/Gigantes.html">
      THOON
     </a>
     A Gigante clubbed to death by the Moirae (Fates) with clubs of bronze in the Giant-War.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThoosa.html">
      THOOSA
     </a>
     A sea-nymph loved by the god Poseidon who bore him the Cyclops Polyphemus.
    </p>
    <p>
     THRACE (Thrake) A mysterious goddess-witch of Thrace who bore sons to Cronus, Zeus and the giant Briareus.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThrassa.html">
      THRASSA
     </a>
     A Thracian nymph daughter of the god Ares.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Daimon/Thrasos.html">
      THRASUS
     </a>
     (Thrasos) The spirit of rash action and insolence.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NymphaiThriai.html">
      THRIAE
     </a>
     (Thriai) Three prophetic nymphs of Mount Parnassus in Phocis. They presided over the rustic art of divination by pebbles and were the goddesses of birds of omen. They were sometimes depicted with the bodies of bees.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThronie.html">
      THRONIA
     </a>
     A Thracian Naiad-nymph loved by the god Poseidon.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThyia.html">
      THYIA
     </a>
     A Naiad-nymph of Mount Parnassus loved by the god Apollo. She was the first to celebrate the orgies of Dionysus at Delphi.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThymbris.html">
      THYMBRIS
     </a>
     A prophetic Arcadian nymph who was the mother of the mantic god Pan by Zeus.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Georgikos/Thyone.html">
      THYONE
     </a>
     The mother of the god Dionysus who was first destroyed by the lighning-bolts of Zeus but later recovered by her son from the underworld and brought to Olympus as a goddess.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheThysa.html">
      THYSA
     </a>
     The goddess-nymph of the frenzied state of the celebrants of the Bacchic orgies. She was a daughter of the god Dionysus.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheTiasa.html">
      TIASA
     </a>
     The Naiad-nymph of a Spartan stream.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Khthonios/Erinyes.html">
      TISIPHONE
     </a>
     One of the three Erinyes, goddesses of vengeance.
    </p>
    <figure class="image-main image-main-400">
     <a href="../Gigante/Typhoeus.html">
      <img alt="Typhoeus" class="img-responsive center-block" src="../image/M10.1Typhon.jpg"/>
     </a>
     <figcaption>
      <a href="../Gigante/Typhoeus.html">
       TYPHOEUS
      </a>
     </figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p>
     <a href="../Titan/Titanides.html">
      TITANIDES
     </a>
     The female Titans. The eldest of these were six daughters of Uranus named Rhea, Themis, Thea, Mnemosyne, Tethys, Phoebe and Dione. They remained neutral in the Titan-War and retained their role as minor earth-goddesses. Many of the daughters of the Titans were also called Titanides such as Leto, Eos, Selene, and Hecate.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Titan/Titanes.html">
      TITANS
     </a>
     (Titanes) The six sons of Uranus--Oceanus, Coeus, Crius, Iapetus, Hyperion and Cronus--who deposed their father as king of the cosmos. They were in turn cast down by Zeus and imprisoned in the pit of Tartarus--except for Oceanus who remained neutral. Some of their sons and grandsons such as Atlas, Prometheus and Helius were also called Titans.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     TITHONUS (Tithonos) A handsome shepherd-prince carried off by the dawn-goddess Eos. She obtained the gift of immortality for him but forget to ask for eternal youth and in time he shrivelled up and was transformed into a cicada.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheTithorea.html">
      TITHOREA
     </a>
     The eponymous Dryad-nymph of the Phocian town of Tithorea.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Georgikos/SatyroiTityroi.html">
      TITYRI
     </a>
     (Tityroi) A tribe of flute-playing, rustic daemones related to the Satyrs. They were companions of the god Dionysus.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Gigante/GiganteTityos.html">
      TITYUS
     </a>
     (Tityos) A giant who was slain by Apollo when he attempted to violate the goddess Leto and afterwards sentenced to eternal torment in Hades.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Titan/Kyklopes.html">
      TRACHIUS
     </a>
     (Trakhios) One of the metal-working Cyclops-giants.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Georgikos/EleusiniosTriptolemos.html">
      TRIPTOLEMUS
     </a>
     (Triptolemos) The agricultural-god of the sowing and the threshing of grain. He was a  champion of the goddess Demeter who gave him a winged, serpent-drawn chariot to spread knowldedge of agriculture throughout the world.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheTriteia.html">
      TRITEIA
     </a>
     The warlike sea-nymph of the Achaean town of Triteia who was a consort of the god Ares.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Pontios/Triton.html">
      TRITON
     </a>
     The sea-god herald of the god Poseidon who calmed the seas with the trumpeting of his conch-shell horn. He was a merman with one, or a pair of, fish-tails in place of legs.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     TRITONIDES Sea-nymphs daughters of the god Triton.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheTritonis.html">
      TRITONIS
     </a>
     The goddess of Lake Tritonis in Libya. She was the mother of the Libyan Athena.
    </p>
    <figure class="image-main image-main-300">
     <a href="../Olympios/Zeus.html">
      <img alt="Zeus" class="img-responsive center-block" src="../image/K1.1Zeus.jpg"/>
     </a>
     <figcaption>
      <a href="../Olympios/Zeus.html">
       ZEUS
      </a>
     </figcaption>
    </figure>
    <a href="../Pontios/Tritones.html">
     TRITONS
    </a>
    (Tritones) Fish-tailed sea-daemones. They were named after the similarly-shaped god Triton. Some of the Tritons were monstrous creatures with red-eyes, scaly  skin, seaweed hair, sharp claws and shark's teeth.
    <p>
     <a href="../Titan/Hekatonkheires.html">
      TRITOPATORES
     </a>
     The "three fathers", ancient wind-gods responsible for the generation and delivery of new souls.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Georgikos/EleusiniosTrokhilos.html">
      TROCHILUS
     </a>
     (Trokhilos) The demigod of the mill-stone used for grinding wheat into flour. He was one of the Eleusinian attendants of the goddess Demeter.
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="heading">
      TROGLODYTES
     </span>
     A tribe of underground-dwelling men.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Khthonios/Trophonios.html">
      TROPHONIUS
     </a>
     (Trophonios) A man swallowed up by the earth and transformed into an oracular daemon.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Daimon/Tykhe.html">
      TYCHE
     </a>
     (Tykhe) The goddess of the good fortune and luck.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Georgikos/SatyroiOrthanes.html">
      TYCHON
     </a>
     (Tykhon) A phallic fertility-daemon.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Gigante/Typhoeus.html">
      TYPHOEUS
     </a>
     (Typhoeus) A monstrous storm-giant with a hundred bestial heads, serpentine legs and giant wings. He besieged heaven, wrestled Zeus for his lightning bolts, tore out his sinews and locked him away in a cave. The god was restored by his sons and, striking Typhoeus down with his thunderbolts, imprisoned him in Tartarus or beneath the volcano Etna.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Gigante/Typhoeus.html">
      TYPHON
     </a>
     (Typhon) Another name for the monster Typhoeus.
    </p>
    <hr/>
    <p id="U">
     <a href="../Nymphe/NymphaiHyperboreiai.html">
      UPIS
     </a>
     (Oupis) A Hyperborean nymph in the retinue of the goddess Artemis.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Ouranios/MousaOurania.html">
      URANIA
     </a>
     (Ourania) The godess Muse of astronomy.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Protogenos/Ouranos.html">
      URANUS
     </a>
     (Ouranos) The primordial god of the sky--a solid, bronze dome which enclosed the flat earth and upper half of the cosmos. Uranus was the first ruler of the universe who was castrated and deposed by his son Cronus.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <hr/>
    <p id="X">
     <a href="../Ther/HippoiBaliosXanthos.html">
      XANTHUS
     </a>
     (Xanthos) One of the immortal horses owned by the hero Peleus and his son Achilles.
    </p>
    <hr/>
    <p id="Z">
     <a href="../Georgikos/Zagreus.html">
      ZAGREUS
     </a>
     (Zagreos) The first-born son of Zeus who was seated on the throne of heaven by his father as a babe and armed with thunderbolts. The Titan-gods stole into Olympus, distracted the child with toys and dismembered him with  their knives. Athena recovered his heart and he was reborn through Semele as the god Dionysus.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Daimon/Zelos.html">
      ZELUS
     </a>
     (Zelos) The personification of rivalry. He and his three siblings were henchmen of the god Zeus.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Titan/AnemosZephyros.html">
      ZEPHYRUS
     </a>
     (Zephyros) The god of the gentle west-wind and the season of spring.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Olympios/Zeus.html">
      ZEUS
     </a>
     The king of the gods, greatest of the twelve Olympians. He was the god of the heavens, weather, fate, and kings. His weapon was the flaming thunderbolt.
     <img alt="Illustrated" src="../image/eye.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Nymphe/NympheZeuxippe.html">
      ZEUXIPPE
     </a>
     An Athenian Naiad-nymph, mother of the Argonaut Butes.
    </p>
