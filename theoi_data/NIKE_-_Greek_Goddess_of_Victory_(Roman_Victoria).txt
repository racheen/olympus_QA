    <p>
     NIKE (Nicé) was the winged goddess of victory--victory both in war and in peaceful competition. When
     <a href="../Olympios/Zeus.html">
      Zeus
     </a>
     was gathering allies at the start of the
     <a href="../Titan/Titanes.html">
      Titan War
     </a>
     , Styx brought her four children Nike (Victory), Zelos (Rivalry), Kratos (Cratus Strength) and Bia (Force) into the god's service. Nike was appointed his charioteer and together the four became sentinels of Zeus' throne.
    </p>
    <p>
     Nike was depicted in ancient Greek vase painting with a variety of attributes including a wreath or sash to crown a victor, an
     <em>
      oinochoe
     </em>
     and
     <em>
      phiale
     </em>
     (bowl and cup) for libations, a
     <em>
      thymiaterion
     </em>
     (incense burner), an altar, and a lyre for the celebration of victory in song.
     <br/>
     In scenes of the War of the Giants she  appears as the charioteer of Zeus. 
  In mosaic art and coins Nike is often shown holding a palm branch as a symbol of victory.
    </p>
    <p>
     Nike was closely identified with the goddess
     <a href="../Olympios/Athena.html">
      Athena
     </a>
     and at times was little more than an attribute of the goddess. Nike was sometimes multiplied into a host of Nikai (Nicae, Victories).
    </p>

    <h2 id="Encyclopedia">
     ENCYCLOPEDIA
    </h2>
    <p>
     NICE (Nikê). 1. The goddess of victory, or, as the Romans called her, Victoria, is described as a daughter of Pallas and Styx, and as a sister of Zelus (zeal), Cratos (strength), and Bia (force). At the time when Zeus entered upon the fight against the Titans, and called upon the gods for assistance, Nice and her two sisters were the first that came forward, and Zeus was so pleased with their readiness, that he caused them ever after to live with him in Olympus. (Hes.
     <em>
      Theog.
     </em>
     382, &amp;c.; Apollod. i. 2. § 2.) Nice had a celebrated temple on the acropolis of Athens, which is still extant and in excellent preservation. (Paus. i. 22. § 4. iii. 15. § 5.) She is often seen represented in ancient works of art, especially together with other divinities, such as Zeus and Athena, and with conquering heroes whose horses she guides. In her appearance she resembles Athena, but has wings, and carries a palm or a wreath, and is engaged in raising a trophy, or in inscribing the victory of the conqueror on a shield. (Paus. v. 10. § 2. 11. §§ 1, 2, .vi. 18. § 1.)
    </p>
    <p>
     2. Nice also occurs as a surname of Athena, under which the goddess had a sanctuary on the acropolis of Megara. (Paus. i. 42. § 4; Eurip.
     <em>
      Ion,
     </em>
     1529.)
    </p>
    <p>
     <em>
      Source: Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology.
     </em>
    </p>

For so did Styx the deathless daughter of Okeanos plan on that day when the Olympian Lightener called all the deathless gods to great Olympos (Olympus), and said that whosoever of the gods would fight with him against the Titanes (Titans), he would not cast him out from his rights, but each should have the office which he had before amongst the deathless gods.     </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-other">
      Suidas s.v. Nike Athena (trans. Suda On Line) (Byzantine Greek Lexicon C10th A.D.) :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "Nike Athena: Lykourgos (Lycurgus) in the [speech]
     <em>
      On the Priestess
     </em>
     [mentions her]. That the xoanon of Nike, wingless, holding a pomegranate in her right hand and a helmet in her left, was worshipped by the Athenians Heliodoros the Periegete has shown in the first book of his
     <em>
      On the Akropolis
     </em>
     . 
  Alternatively [she stands] allegorically for the notion that even winning is completely dependent on thought; for thought contributes to victory, but being thoughtless and impetuous while fighting leads to defeat. When she has wings she symbolizes that aspect of the mind that is sharp and, so to speak, swift-winged; but when she is depicted without wings she represents that aspect of it that is peaceful and quiet and civil, that by which the things of the earth flourish, a boon of which the pomegranate in her right hand is a representation. Just as the helmet in her left [is a representation] of battle. Thus she has the same capability as Athena."
    </p>
    <h4>
     II. PIRAEUS (PEIRAIOS) Town of Attica (Southern Greece)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 1. 1. 3 (trans. Jones) (Greek travelogue C2nd A.D.) :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "[In the temple of Zeus at Peiraios (Piraeus) in Attika :] The images are of bronze; Zeus holds a staff and a Nike."
    </p>
    <h4>
     III. TITANE Town of the Argolis (Southern Greece)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 2. 11. 8  :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "In the gable [of the temple of Asklepios (Asclepius) at Titane in Argolis] at the ends are figures of Herakles and of Nikai (Nicae, Victories)."
    </p>
    <h4>
     IV. SPARTA Chief City of Lacedaemonia (Southern Greece)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 3. 17. 4 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "The west portico [of the temple of Athene (Athena) in Sparta, Lakedaimonia] has two eagles, and upon them are two Nikai (Nicae, Victories). Lysander dedicated them to commemorate both his exploits [war victories]."
    </p>
    <h4>
     V. OLYMPIA Sanctuary in Elis (Southern Greece)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 11. 1 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "In his [the statue of Zeus in his temple at Olympia] right hand he carried a Nike, which, like the statue, is of ivory and gold; she wears a ribbon and--on her head--a garland . . . There are four Nikai (Nicae, Victories), represented as dancing women, one at each foot of the throne, and two others at the base of each foot."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 10. 4 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "At Olympia . . . a Nike, also gilt, is set in about the middle of the pediment [of the temple of Zeus]."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 14. 8 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "[At Olympia] is an altar of . . . . Zeus Purifier, one of Nike."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 17. 3 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "[In the temple of Hera at Olympia :] Here too have been dedicted [statues of] Leto, Tykhe (Tyche), Dionysos and a winged Nike."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 26. 1 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "The Dorian Messenians who received Naupaktos [in a victory in war] from the Athenians dedicated at Olympia the image of Nike (Victory) upon the pillar."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 5. 26. 6 - 7 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "Beside the Athena [statue at Olympia] has been set up a Nike. The Mantineans [of Arkadia] dedicated it, but they do not mention the ware in the inscription. Kalamis (Calamis) is said to have made it without wings in imitation of the wooden image at Athens called Nike Apteron (Wingless Victory)."
    </p>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 6. 18. 1 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "[At Olympia :] There is also a bronze statue of Kratisthenes (Cratisthenes) of Kyrene (Cyrene), on the chariot stand Nike and Kratisthenes himself. It is thus plain that his victory was in the chariot-race."
    </p>
    <h4>
     VI. THESPIAE Town in Boeotia (Central Greece)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-greek">
      Pausanias, Description of Greece 9. 27. 5 :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "Not far from the marketplace [at Thespiae, Boiotia (Boeotia)] is a Nike of bronze and a small temple of the Mousai (Muses). In it are small images made of stone."
    </p>
    <h4>
     VII. SYRAKOUSA Town in Sikelia (Sicily) (Southern Italy)
    </h4>
    <p>
     <span class="quote-latin">
      Cicero, De Natura Deorum 3. 34 (trans. Rackham) (Roman rhetorician C1st B.C.) :
     </span>
     <br/>
     "Also he [the historic Sicilian tyrant Dionysius of Syracuse] have no scruples in removing the little gold images of Nike (Victory) and the gold cups and crowns carried in the outstretched hands of statues [of the gods]."
    </p>
