        <p>
         The plants and flowers of ancient Greece were rich in mythology: ranging from the tale of Persephone and the pomegranate seeds, to the flowery metamorphoses of Hyakinthos, the golden apples of the Hesperides, and the tree-morphing nymph Daphne.

        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Also :
         </span>
         Monkshood, Wolf's-bane
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Akoniton, lykoktonon
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Aconitum napellus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         An herbaceous perennial  plant with  purplish-blue  flowers which grows up to a metre in height. Its leaves and roots are extremely extremely toxic and were used by the ancients as a poison. The  Greek's called it
         <em>
          akoniton
         </em>
         (without dirt) because it grows on rocky ground, and
         <em>
          lykoktonon
         </em>
         (wolf-slaying) because it was traditionally applied by their arrows when hunting wolves.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Ther/KuonKerberos.html">
          Spittle of Kerberos
         </a>
         . Herakles was sent to fetch Kerberos forth from the underworld as one of his twelve labours. The spittle of the beast dripped upon the earth, and from it sprang the first aconite plant.  (Source: Ovid)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <span class="details">
          Poison of Medea.
         </span>
         Theseus once travelled to Athens to present himself to his long lost father King Aegeas. The king's wife Medea,  recognised the youth, and persuaded Aegeas to let her offer him a cup of wine laced with deadly aconite. However, just in time, Aegeas caught sight of the sword  which he had left Theseus to be a mark of his paternity, and dashed the cup from his hands. (Source: Ovid, Plutarch)
        </p>
        <h2>
         ALMOND TREE
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Amugdalea
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Prunus amygdalus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small deciduous tree growing 4 to 9 metres tall. It flowers in pink-white blossoms in the spring and produces an autumn crop of edible nuts in a tough shell.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Attis (born of the almond nut)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Phrygios/Agdistis.html">
          Birth of Attis
         </a>
         . In Phrygia there was born an hermaphroditic deity named Agdistis. The gods were fearful and castrated it creating the goddess Kybele. The genitals were cast upon the earth where they sprouted and grew into an almond tree. Once when the nymph Nana was sitting beneath its branches a nut fell into her lap and impregnated her. The child conceived was Attis, who grew up to became the consort of the Kybele. (Source: Pausanias)
        </p>
        <h2>
         ANEMONE, POPPY or WINDFLOWER, GREEK
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Anemônê
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Anemone coronaria
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A perennial spring-flowering bulb with delicate blood-red flowers. Their seed is carried on the wind, hence the ancient Greek name for the flower, anemone (from anemos, the wind).
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Aphrodite (probably planted in the "Adonis-gardens")
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/AphroditeLoves2.html#Adonis">
          Death of Adonis
         </a>
         . Adonis was a handsome youth loved by the goddess Aphrodite. When he was slain by a wild boar, the goddess created the red anemone flower from his blood. (Source: Ovid)
        </p>
        <h2>
         APPLE TREE
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Mêlon
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Malus domestica
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         The apple-tree was an important orchard fruit of ancient Greece. It was associated with love and marriage.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Hera (assoc. with weddings), Aphrodite (assoc. with love)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="../Olympios/Hera.html">
          Wedding of Hera
         </a>
         . The earth-goddess Gaia produced first apple-tree as a wedding-present for the goddess Hera. This tree of the golden apples was guarded by the three goddess Hesperides. (Source: Apollodorus, Hyginus)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/JudgementParis.html">
          Judgment of Paris.
         </a>
         At the wedding of Peleus and Thetis, Eris the goddess of strife, cast a golden apple addressed to the fairest amongst the goddesses. Aphrodite, Hera and Athene all laid claim to the prize. They were referred by Zeus to the shepherd prince Paris, who awarded the apple to Aphrodite, goddess of love and beauty. (Source: Stasinus, Apollodorus)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 3 :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/AphroditeFavour.html#Hippomenes">
          Melanion &amp; Atalanta
         </a>
         . The beautiful-princess Atalanta was reluctant to wed, and insisted that her suitors best her in a race. Those who failed the contest would be put to death by her father. The youth Melanion (meaning he of the apples) prayed to Aphrodite for help, and the goddess presented him with three golden apples. These he cast before the princess in the race, slowing her down as she stooped to retrieve them and so won the race. (Source: Hesiod, Apollodorus, Ovid)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 4 :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/Epimelides.html">
          Nymphai Epimelides
         </a>
         . The nymph-protectors of apple-orchards.
        </p>
        <h2>
         ASH, MANNA
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Melia
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Fraxinus ornus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small spring-flowering deciduous tree. It secretes a sweet sap known as manna in July and August which was harvested by the ancients. Manna was believed to be closely related to honey (the word for both was
         <em>
          meli
         </em>
         in Greek). The tree was said to have been first sprung from the blood of heaven, and its manna was often described as the sky-fallen juice of the stars. 
                        The stem of the young ash was in the crafting spear-shafts.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Zeus (manna juice), Kouretes &amp; Ares (ash-spears)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/NymphaiMeliai.html">
          Nymphai Meliai
         </a>
         . The Meliai were the Nymphs of the manna ash-tree who were born from the blood of the castrated Ouranos which splattered upon the earth. They were entrusted with the raising of the infant Zeus whom they fed on the honey and the milk of the goat Amaltheia. The Meliai were also the ancestresses of mankind. (Source: Hesiod, Apollodorus, Callimachus, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Georgikos/KentaurosKheiron.html">
          Pelian Ash Spear
         </a>
         . The spear of Akhilleus, the great hero of the Trojan War, was crafted  by the centaur Kheiron for his father Peleus from an ash  growing on Mount Pelion. (Source: Homer, Apollodorus, et al)
        </p>
        <h2>
         ASPHODEL, COMMON
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Asphodelos
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Asphodelus ramosus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A spring-flowering perennial with grey-green leaves and pale pink-grey-white flowers. The tubers are edible.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Haides (asphodel planted around tombs), Persephone &amp; Hekate (statues of these goddesses were adorned with asphodel on the island of Rhodes)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Asphodel Plains
         </span>
         . This ghostly grey plant was believed to dominate the fields of the land of the dead. It was regarded as the food of the dead. (Source: Homer)

        <h2>
         BARLEY
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Krithê
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Hordeum vulgare
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         An important grain-crop in the ancient word, second only to wheat.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Demeter (her sacred Eleusinian drink was made of barley, honey and mint)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/DemeterWrath.html#Askalabos">
          Metamorphosis Askalabos
         </a>
         . When Demeter was searching for Persephone, she stopped to rest at a farmer's cottage and quenched her thirst with a sweet barley-drink. A boy name Askalabos mocked her hunger and in anger the goddess cast the drink upon him, transforming him into a lizard. The flecks of barley became the creature's spots. (Source: Ovid)
        </p>

        <h2>
         CEDAR, PRICKLY or JUNIPER, PRICKLY
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Kedros
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Juniperus oxycedrus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small tree with needle like leaves which grows to a height of 10 to 15 metres. The juniper's berries ripen to an orange-red, and the male cones are yellow.  The tree is prized for its wood. The famous Phoenician cedar of Lebanon, also found in Crete, is another species.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Artemis (there was a sanctuary of Artemis Kedreatis, Lady of the Cedar, near Orkhomenos where the cult image was set in the tree)
        </p>
        <h2>
         CELERY, WILD and PARSLEY
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Selinon
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Apium graveolens and Petroselinum sativum
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         The Greeks did not distinguish between parsley and celery. Wreaths of wild celery or parsley were worn by mourners at funerals and hung in tombs.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Zeus (victors of his Nemean Games were crowned with wild celery), Poseidon (victors of his Isthmian Games were later also crowned with  celery, replacing the pine wreath)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Death of Opheltes.
         </span>
         The nurse of the infant prince Opheltes lay him in a bed of wild celery as she gave directions to the heroes of the Seven Against Thebes. A serpent came forth and killed the baby. The warriors then founded the Nemean Games in his honour with a victor's wreath made from his celery death-bed. (Source: Apollodorus, et al)
        </p>
        <h2>
         CHASTE TREE or WITHY
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Lygos, agnos
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Vitex agnus castus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small deciduous tree or large shrub with hemp-like leaves and branched clusters of purple flowers. Its stems were used to make wicker items. The plant was believed to calm sexual appetites and was used as a medicine for women.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Hera (assoc. with marital chastity, sacred tree in her Samian temple), Hestia (virgin priestesses carried chaste-tree stems), Artemis (Spartan statue bound in withy stems), Demeter (matrons strew their beds with flowers of the tree during the Thesmophoria festival)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/Hera.html">
          Birth of Hera
         </a>
         . The goddess Hera was born and nursed beneath a sacred chaste-tree on the island of Samos. (Source: Pausanias)
        </p>

        <h2>
         CHERRY TREE, CORNELIAN
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Also :
         </span>
         European cornel
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Kraneia
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Cornus mas
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small, deciduous tree which grows to a height of 5 to 12 metres tall. It has yellow spring blossoms and bright red fruit which ripen in late summer. The fruit is edible but has an acidic tang. They ancients mostly used them as swine-feed. The tree was also prized for its wood.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/NymphaiHamadryades.html">
          Hamadryas Kraneia
         </a>
         . The Hamadryad nymph of the Cornelian cherry tree. (Source: Athenaeus)
        </p>

        <h2>
         CROCUS, SAFFRON
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Krokos
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Crocus sativus
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A late-summer and autumn blooming perennial whose yellow stigmas are the source of saffron, a food spice and expensive yellow dye.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Hermes
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Metamorphosis  Krokos.
         </span>
         A boy loved by the god Hermes. After his accidental death the god transformed him into the saffron flower. Its red stems were desribed as his spilt blood. According to others Krokos was metamorphosed into the flower following the death of his love, the Nymph Smilax. (Source: Ovid, Pliny, et al)

         </a>
         . The goddess Persephone and her companion Nymphs were gathering  rose, crocus, violet, iris, lily and larkspur blooms in a springtime meadow when she was abducted by the god Haides. (Source: Homeric Hymns)

         </span>

         </span>
         . Zeus spied the Phoenician princess Europa gathering flowers in a spring-time meadow. He transformed himself into a bull and breathed a crocus from his mouth to drawn her near and so carry her away. (Source: Hesiod)

        <h2>
         FIR, GRECIAN and FIR, SILVER
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Also :
         </span>
         Turkish fir and Trojan fir
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Elatê
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Abies cephalonica, A. alba, A. nordmanniana, A. equi-trojani
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         Firs are mountain growing pyrmaidal-shaped conifers. The silver fir grows to a height of 100 to 150 feet and the Grecian fir around 80. Their tall cones ripen around October. The juice of the silver fir (a turpentine-like oil) was mixed by the Greeks with new wine to make it keep. Fir wood was used for building.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Dionysos (his devotees wielded fir-cone tipped staffs), Kybele (a sacred silver fir was decorated at the centre of her mountain orgies), Eileithyia (the gum of the silver fir called the menses of Eileithyia, perhaps with a medicinal use in childbirth)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/Dionysos.html">
          Thyrsos of Dionysos
         </a>
         . The thyrsos of Dionysos was a fir- (or pine) cone tipped staff. The fir-cone was a symbol of the god's phallus.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Phrygios/Attis.html">
          Metamorphosis Attis
         </a>
         . Attis was a handsome youth loved by the goddess Kybele. However when she discovered that he had been unfaithful, she forced him to castrate himself and transformed him into a silver fir. The tree was decorated at the centre of her orgiastic rituals, its phallic cone representing the castrated members of her lover. (Sources: Pausanias, Ovid)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 3 :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/Dryades.html">
          Nymphai Oreiades
         </a>
         . At the birth of a mountain Nymph a lofty silver fir or holm oak sprung up from the earth and withered when she died. (Homeric Hymns)
        </p>

        <h2>
         HERBS, VARIOUS
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Pharmakon (singular), pharmaka (plural)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Various (some were purely mythical)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         In ancient Greek, the word
         <em>
          pharmakeia
         </em>
         referred to the use of herbal drugs, potions and charms. It included not only the physician's art, but also magic (i.e. the use of superstitious charms and spells by the average man), witchcraft (the harmful use of magic) and the concoction of poisons. The Greek word for witch was
         <em>
          pharmakis
         </em>
         . The ancient pharmacological writer Dioscurides describes the medicinal properties of herbs and other plants in great detail. Witchcraft is mentioned in passing by many poets, but the most descriptive account is surely to be found in the
         <em>
          Golden Ass
         </em>
         of  Apuleius.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Asklepios (god of medicine) ; Hekate (goddess of witchcraft)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Titan/TitanKronos.html">
          Emetic of Kronos
         </a>
         . The Titan Kronos devoured each of his children as soon as they were born, except Zeus, who was safely hidden away by his mother Rheia. When the god was grown he sought the aid of the goddess Metis or Gaia who served Kronos an herbal emetic which caused the Titan to disgorge his divine sibligns: Poseidon, Haides, Hera, Demeter and Hestia. (Source: Hesiod, Apollodorus, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Gigante/Gigantes.html">
          Herb of the Gigantes
         </a>
         . The Gigantes were monstrous sons of Gaia the earth who once made war on the gods of Olympos. Gaia had heard from an oracle that her sons were destined to be destroyed and  so searched  the world for a magical herb which would grant them immortality. Zeus, however, forbade the Sun, Moon and Dawn to shine, and found the drug before her. (Source: Apollodorus)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 3 :
         </span>
         <a href="Pontios/Glaukos.html">
          Herb of Glaukos
         </a>
         . Glaukos was a fisherman from the Boiotian  town of Anthedon who found a magical herb growing near the shore. He ate it and was transformed into a fish-tailed sea-god. (Source: Pausanias, Ovid, Nonnus)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 4 :
         </span>
         <a href="Georgikos/KentaurosKheiron.html">
          Medicines of Kheiron
         </a>
         . Kheiron was a learned centaur who made his home on  Magnesian Mount Pelion. He was the first to discover the use of medicinal herbs and instructed many heroes in the craft, including the famous Asklepios. (Source: Homer, Pindar, Apollodorus, Aelian, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 5 :
         </span>
         <a href="Ouranios/Asklepios.html">
          Medicines of Asklepios
         </a>
         . Asklepios was the mythical founder of medicine. As a youth he was instructed by the centaur Kheiron in the use of herbal remedies, but went on to perfect the art. He became so skilled  that he could even bring the dead back to life. The ancient guild of doctors, who were known as the Asklepiades, claimed descent from him. (Source: Homer, Pindar, Apollodorus, Aelian, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 6 :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Herb of Polyidos.
         </span>
         Polyidos was a seer summoned to the court of King Minos of Krete to find his missing son Glaukos. He found the boy drowned in a vat of honey, and was then locked by the king in a cell with the body and commanded to bring him back to life. When a serpent crawled toward the corpse, Polyidos killed it, and watched as its mate appeared with a magical herb and restored it to life. Using this plant he then revived the Glaukos and was freed. (Source: Apollodorus, Hyginus, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 7 :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/NympheMoria.html">
          Herb of Moria
         </a>
         . Moria was a Lydian nymph whose brother Tylos was killed by a giant serpent. The hero Damasen killed the beast, but afterwards, as Moria was watching, its mate appeared and restored it to life with a magical herb. She then used the same  to revive her own dead brother. (Source: Nonnus)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 8 :
         </span>
         <a href="Titan/Kirke.html">
          Witchcraft of Kirke
         </a>
         . The witch Kirke was titled polypharmakos (of the many herbs) by Homer. She was said to have been the first to devise magical herbal potions, and used these magical concoctions to transform men into beasts, including the comrades of Odysseus, the Italian youth Pikos, and the nymph Skylla. (Source: Homer, Ovid, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 9 :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Witchcraft of Medea.
         </span>
         The witch Medea was skilled in the use of magical herbs. She made a fire-resistant salve for the hero Jason when he was forced to face the fire-breathing bulls of king Aeetes ; she put the serpent guardian of the Golden Fleece to sleep with a potion ; restored Jason's father and also the Hyades to youth by boiling them in a cauldron with magical herbs ; and various other magical wonders. Medea was said to have left her basket of Kolkhian herbs on Mount Pelion where they sprouted for the use of later Thessalian withces. She also later tried to kill the hero Theseus with poison aconite. (Source: Apollonius Rhodius, Valerius Flaccus, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          See also :
         </span>
         Aconite, Hellebore, Hemlock, Mint and Moly (separate entries)
        </p>

        <h2>
         IRIS, DWARF and IRIS, SWEET
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Agallis, iris
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Iris attica, I. pallida
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A spring flowering bulb with purple, blue or mauve blooms.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Khthonios/HaidesPersephone1.html">
          Bouquet of Persephone
         </a>
         . The goddess Persephone and her companion Nymphs were gathering rose, crocus, violet, dwarf iris (
         <em>
          agallis
         </em>
         ), lily and larkspur blooms in a springtime meadow when she was abducted by the god Haides. (Source: Homeric Hymns)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Pontios/Iris.html">
          Goddess Iris
         </a>
         . The sweet iris was named by the Greeks after the goddess of the rainbow.
        </p>
        <h2>
         IVY, COMMON
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Kissos
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Hedera helix
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A creeping vine which flowers in autumn and whose black berries ripen in late winter.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Dionysos (ivy garlands were worn by celebrants of the god's orgies and ivy was used to decorate their thyrsos-staffs)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/DionysosMyths.html#Birth">
          Nursing of Dionysos
         </a>
         . After the birth of Dionysos his jealous stepmother Hera sought to destroy him. So his nurses, the Nymphai Nysiades, screened his crib with ivy-leaves to keep him safely hidden. Kisseis (the lady of the ivy) was the name of one of these Nymphs. (Source: Ovid)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <a href="Georgikos/Korymbos.html">
          Korymbos
         </a>
         . The son of one of the nurses of Dionysos. He was the god of the fruit of the ivy (in Greek korymbos). (Source: Nonnus)
        </p>

        <h2>
         LARKSPUR, ROCKET
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Hyakinthos
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Delphinium ajacis
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A late spring, early summer flowering perennial with tall stems of blue flowers.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Apollon (the plant was probably used in the god's Hyakinthia festival held near Sparta in early summer)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 1 :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/ApollonLoves.html#Hyakinthos">
          Metamorphosis Hyakinthos
         </a>
         . Hyakinthos was a handsome, young Spartan prince loved by the gods Apollon and Zephyros. Zephyros grew jealous  and one day when Apollon was playing quoits with the boy he caught up the disc with his windy breath and struck him in the head. As Hyakinthos lay dying, Apollon caused the larkspur or iris flower to spring forth from his blood, inscribing it with the Greek words "ai ai", alas, alas. (Sources: Ovid, Philostratus, Pausanias, et al)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 2 :
         </span>
         <span class="details2">
          Death of Aias
         </span>
         . Aias (or Ajax) was a Greek hero of the Trojan war. After the armour of Akhilleus was awarded to Odysseus he went mad and threw himself upon his own sword. The larkspur flower sprang  from his blood, its petals inscribed with the "ai" of his name. (Source: Ovid)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Myth 3 :
         </span>
         <a href="Khthonios/HaidesPersephone1.html">
          Bouquet of Persephone
         </a>
         . The goddess Persephone and her companion Nymphs were gathering rose, crocus, violet, iris, lily and larkspur blooms in a springtime meadow when she was abducted by the god Haides. (Source: Homeric Hymns)
         <br/>
         (NB The flower now called the hyacinth is not the same as the Greek hyakinthos. Rather it is identified with the larkspur. In the image right - click on thumbnail - ,  the letters AI are typed beside the letter shapes on the central petal.)
        </p>
        <h2>
         LAUREL or BAY, SWEET
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Daphnê
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Laurus nobilis
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A small tree growing to a maximum height of 40 feet. It flowers in late spring and produces purplish black berries in the fall. The aromatic leaves of the bay are edible and are / were used in cooking.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Apollon (the god and victors of his Phthian Games were crowned with wreaths of laurel ; he was titled Daphnaios) ; Artemis (she had sacred laurel groves and was titled Daphnaia)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Nymphe/NympheDaphne.html">
          Metamorphosis Daphne
         </a>
         . Daphne was an Arkadian Nymphe loved by the god Apollon. When he pursued her, she fled and transformed into a laurel tree to escape him. The plant was ever after sacred to the god. (Sources: Pausanias, Ovid, Hyginus)
        </p>
        <h2>
         LETTUCE, PRICKLY
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Thridax
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Lactuca serriola
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         The ancient Greeks cultivated the wild prickly lettuce. The plant has tall stalks with elongated leaves,  yellow flowers and feathery seeds. The ball-shaped lettuce of today is a derivitive species (Lactuca sativa).
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Sacred to :
         </span>
         Aphrodite (the plant was associated with impotency)
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Olympios/AphroditeLoves2.html#Adonis">
          Death of Adonis
         </a>
         . Adonis was a handsome youth loved by the goddess Aphrodite. He was slain by a wild boar in a bed of lettuce, or was laid out amongst the plants by the goddess following his death. The lettuce was therefore regarded as the plant of the death of love, and so of impotency. Others say that the baby Adonis was hidden in a lettuce bed by the goddess following his birth from the trunk of the tree Myrrha. (Source: Athenaeus)
        </p>
        <h2>
         LILY, MADONNA
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span class="details">
          Greek :
         </span>
         Leirion, krinon
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Species :
         </span>
         Lilium candidum
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Description :
         </span>
         A white-flowered spring perennial which grows to a height of up to 1.2 metres.
         <br/>
         <span class="details">
          Mythology :
         </span>
         <a href="Khthonios/HaidesPersephone1.html">
          Bouquet of Persephone
         </a>
         . The goddess Persephone and her companion Nymphs were gathering rose, crocus, violet, dwarf iris, lily (
         <em>
          leirion
         </em>
         ) and larkspur blooms in a springtime meadow when she was abducted by the god Haides. (Source: Homeric Hymns)
