    <h2>
     A LIST OF THE FANTASTIC CREATURES FROM ANCIENT GREEK LEGEND
    </h2>
    <p>
     The following creatures were all regarded as species of animal.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SatyroiLibyes.html">
      AEGIPAN, LIBYAN
     </a>
     (Aigipan Libys) Goat-horned and legged men who lived in the forest of Mount Atlas.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Amphisbainai.html">
      AMPHISBAENA
     </a>
     (Amphisbaina) A Saharan serpent with two heads, one at each end of its body.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/MyrmekesIndikoi.html">
      ANT, INDIAN
     </a>
     (Myrmex Indikos) Gigantic insects which guarded deposits of gold of the Indian deserts.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Basiliskoi.html">
      BASILISK
     </a>
     (Basiliskos) A deadly African serpent which killed by touch and poisonous breath.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/TauroiAithiopikoi.html">
      BULL, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Tauros Aithiopikos) A gigantic, aggressive African bull whose red hides were impervious to steel.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Katoblepones.html">
      CATOBLEPAS
     </a>
     (Katobleps) A bull-like African beast whose downward looking head, when raised, could killa man by gaze or with its noxious breath.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/KeteaIndikoi.html">
      CETUS, INDIAN
     </a>
     (Ketos Indikos) Fabulous half-animal, half-fish sea-monsters which inhabited the Indian ocean. They included fish-tailed lions, bears, wolves, rams and spine-haired mermaids.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Leukrokotai.html">
      CROCOTTA
     </a>
     (Krokottas) A hyena-like Aethiopian beast with a powerful jaw-plate in place of teeth which imitated the human voice to lure its prey.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/DrakonesAithiopikoi.html">
      DRAGON, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Drakones Aithiopikoi) Gigantic African serpents.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/DrakonesIndikoi.html">
      DRAGON, INDIAN
     </a>
     (Drakon Indikos) Giant Indian serpents which preyed on elephants.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/DrakonesPhrygioi.html">
      DRAGON, PHRYGIAN
     </a>
     (Drakon Phrygios) Giant Anatolian serpents which stood upright on their tails to snare birds from the air.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Eale.html">
      EALE
     </a>
     (Eale) An antelope-like beast with tusks and moveable horns.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Grypes.html">
      GRIFFIN
     </a>
     (Gryps) An eagle-headed lion native to the mountains of Scythia in North-Eastern Europe.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Leukrokotai.html">
      LEUCROCOTTA
     </a>
     (Leukrokottas) A hyena-like Aethiopian beast with a powerful jaw-plate in place of teeth which imitated the human voice to lure its prey.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Mantikhoras.html">
      MANTICORE
     </a>
     (Mantikhora) A winged Persian monster with the head of a man, the body of a lion and a spiked missile-throwing tail.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/HippoiMonokerata.html">
      MONOCERATUS
     </a>
     (Hippos Monokeratos) Or unicorn, a magical single-horned horse native to India.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Neades.html">
      NEADES
     </a>
     A giant beast native to the island of Samos whose roar could split open the ground.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Onokentauroi.html">
      ONOCENTAUR
     </a>
     (Onokentauros) An African animal which was a cross between a man and an ass.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/PegasoiAithiopikoi.html">
      PEGASUS, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Pegasos Aithiopikos) A winged Ethiopian horse equipped with horns.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Phoinix.html">
      PHOENIX
     </a>
     (Phoinix) A fabulous golden-red bird whose feathers shone with the light of the sun.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SatyrosAithiopikos.html">
      SATYR, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Satyros Aithiopikos) A phantom satyr which plagued an African town.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SatyroiNesioi.html">
      SATYR, ISLAND
     </a>
     (Satyroi Nesioi) Ape-like satyrs native to islands off the North African coast.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SatyrosLemnios.html">
      SATYR, LEMNIAN
     </a>
     (Satyros Lemnios) A satyr which consorted with a woman of the island of Lemnos.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SatyroiLibyes.html">
      SATYR, LIBYAN
     </a>
     (Satyroi Libyes) Monkey-like satyrs which lived in the forest of Mount Atlas in North-West Africa.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Skolopendra.html">
      SCOLOPENDRA
     </a>
     (Skolopendra) A gargantuan sea-monster with hair extending from its nostrils, a flat crayfish-like tail and rows of webbed feet lining each of its flanks.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/OphiesPteretoi.html">
      SERPENT, WINGED
     </a>
     (Ophis Pteretos) A feathery-winged serpent which guarded the rich myrrh fields of Arabia.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/SphinxesAithiopikoi.html">
      SPHINX, ETHIOPIAN
     </a>
     (Sphinx Aithiopikos) Women-headed African lions.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Tritones.html">
      TRITON TANAGRAN
     </a>
     (Triton) A monstrous sea daemon with the upper body of a man and the tail of a fish. It was a fearsome, scaly monster with sharp carnivorous teeth.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/HippoiMonokerata.html">
      UNICORN
     </a>
     (Monokeratos) A white Indian horse equipped with a single, magical horn. The Greek name for the creature was Monoceratus ("One-Horn").
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/WormsIndos.html">
      WORM, INDOS
     </a>
     Gigantic white worms which inhabited the Indos river.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="../Thaumasios/Eale.html">
      YALE
     </a>
     (Eale) An antelope-like beast with tusks and moveable horns.
    </p>
